## The one-liner

Agstya.Energy helped operators answer three hard questions in one place: **Where should I build a charging station? What will it cost to build and run? And how should I price each session once it's live?**

---

## The problem

Putting up an EV charger sounds simple until you have to decide *where*. Operators were making six-figure siting decisions on gut feel and scattered spreadsheets — guessing at traffic, grid capacity, land cost, and competing stations. A bad location doesn't just underperform; it strands capital in concrete and hardware that can't be moved.

We saw three connected pain points:

1. **Siting was a guess.** No single view of demand, grid access, footfall, and competition.
2. **Planning was opaque.** Build and operating costs lived in disconnected estimates, so payback was hard to model before committing.
3. **Pricing was static.** Once live, stations charged flat rates regardless of how busy they were — leaving revenue and utilization on the table.

## The solution

A single platform that walked an operator from "I have a region in mind" to "here's where to build, what it costs, and how to price it."

### 1. Optimal site recommendation
Instead of a blank map, the platform surfaced **ranked candidate locations** scored on the factors that matter — traffic and footfall, grid proximity and capacity, land/lease cost, and nearby competition. The design goal was to make the *why* legible: every recommended site showed the factors behind its score so the decision was defensible, not a black box.

**Key UX decisions:**
- Lead with a ranked shortlist, not raw data, to reduce decision paralysis.
- Make every score expandable into its contributing factors — trust comes from transparency.
- [Map-first vs. list-first layout — note which you chose and why.]

### 2. Cost & planning assistant
Once a site was selected, the platform assembled an estimate of **build cost and operating cost**, turning a scattered spreadsheet exercise into a guided flow. Operators could adjust assumptions and immediately see the effect on payback.

**Key UX decisions:**
- Progressive disclosure: sensible defaults first, fine-grained inputs on demand.
- Show payback / ROI as the headline number — that's the decision operators actually make.
- [Any templates, presets, or scenario-comparison features you designed.]

### 3. Traffic-based dynamic pricing
After installation, the platform recommended **session pricing that flexed with demand** — higher during peak traffic, lower in quiet windows — to lift both utilization and revenue.

**Key UX decisions:**
- Give operators control with guardrails (suggested price + min/max bounds) rather than full automation they wouldn't trust.
- Visualize the demand pattern so a pricing recommendation felt earned, not imposed.
