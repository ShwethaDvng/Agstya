UX Rationale — Flow 01

Lead with a ranked answer, scored on what kills projects.

Five criteria that actually decide a site

Suitability is built from the factors operators lose sleep over: legal status of the land, available grid load, terrain feasibility, foot traffic, and ROI. The map opens pre-scored so the first thing you see is a defensible shortlist.

Legal risk gets equal billing

Disputed-land checks sit alongside demand and ROI — a high-traffic plot with a contested title is surfaced as the liability it is, not buried.

Every score is glass-box

Selecting a site breaks the number into all five criteria, with weak factors flagged in red. Transparency is what lets an operator defend the choice to an investor or board.

One object, two views

Map pin and list row are the same selection and stay in sync — spatial thinkers click the map, comparison thinkers scan the list.

UX Rationale — Flow 02

Make payback the headline — that's the real decision.

Sensible defaults, deeper on demand

The planner opens with a realistic config so a first-time operator gets a credible number in seconds, then exposes the levers — count, charger type, rent, utilization — for the ones who want to tune.

One number above all

Capex, opex and revenue feed the question operators actually ask: when do I make my money back? Payback period gets the largest, most prominent treatment.

Live feedback closes the loop

Every slider recomputes instantly, turning a static spreadsheet into a what-if conversation.

Honest about risk

Net flips to red the moment a configuration loses money — the UI never hides a bad setup behind optimistic styling.

UX Rationale — Flow 03

Recommend a price — but keep the operator in control.

Show the pattern that earns the price

The demand curve is on screen before any price is suggested. A higher peak-hour rate then reads as earned by visible demand, not imposed by a black box.

Make the upside undeniable

A side-by-side of static vs. dynamic daily revenue, plus a single uplift figure, answers the only question that matters: is this worth turning on?

Suggestion, not autopilot

Operators distrust pricing they can't see. Toggling between modes — rather than forcing automation — builds the trust needed before they hand over control.

Price resolves to the tile, not the city

Pricing is spatial as well as temporal: the city is split into demand tiles, so a charger in a hot block earns more than one three streets away. The heatmap lets operators see — and click — exactly where price concentrates.

UX Rationale — Flow 04

Turn the charge price into a market operators can hedge.

One number, traded both ways

The dynamic price from Flow 03 becomes a public index (CHRG). Operators and fleets can take a Long or Short view — the same primitive a stock has — to hedge exposure to volatile energy and demand.

Familiar mental model

Ticker, price chart, stake and P&L borrow conventions traders already know, so there's almost zero learning curve for the financial users this feature targets.

Commit, then watch it resolve

Open a position, then step the market tick-by-tick to see P&L move live — the cause-and-effect loop that makes a speculative product feel trustworthy rather than opaque.

Risk shown honestly

P&L turns red on a loss and a persistent disclaimer frames it as simulated — the design never disguises downside or implies guaranteed return.
